Game-Theoretic Autonomous Vehicle Path Coordination with Limited Communication

Nicole Cruz1*, Jose Fuentes1*, Leonardo Bobadilla1,
1 Florida International University
* Equal contribution

Abstract

The coordination of autonomous vehicles in communication-constrained environments is vital for tasks such as search and rescue, maritime surveillance, and disaster response. Traditional path-planning approaches often rely on extensive communication to ensure collision avoidance and optimal trajectory selection, which is not feasible in adversarial or bandwidth-limited settings. This paper introduces a game-theoretic framework for multi-vehicle coordination, leveraging Nash Equilibria and approximation algorithms to enable effective decision-making with minimal communication overhead. We explore three limited communication strategies: a ⅔-Well-Supported Nash Equilibrium (WSNE) algorithm, a ¾-WSNE algorithm, and a proposed two-way communication extension based on the ¾-WSNE algorithm, dubbed "¾-WSNE." These methods are evaluated using both simulated Voronoi roadmaps and real-world experiments with Autonomous Surface Vehicles (ASVs). Our results demonstrate that strategic path selection can be achieved with minimal information exchange while ensuring collision avoidance and near-optimal path efficiency. This work contributes to the development of scalable, decentralized coordination strategies for autonomous vehicles operating in communication-constrained environments.

Methodology

This coordination framework for autonomous vehicles operates through a three-component process: (i) Equilibrium Approximation, where agents utilize one of three communication-constrained game-theoretic algorithms—¾-WSNE, Two-Way ¾-WSNE, or ⅔-WSNE—to compute near-optimal mixed strategies for path selection; (ii) Strategic Path Selection, where agents construct roadmaps and payoff matrices accounting for collision penalties and individual cost functions, then independently evaluate and select probabilistic paths based on computed strategies; (iii) Communication-Aware Execution, where agents simulate or execute the selected paths with minimal information exchange, balancing payoff maximization with safety and efficiency in both simulated and real-world environments.

Voronoi Roadmap Simulation

We first evaluate our coordination algorithms using Voronoi roadmap simulations in a 2D environment with multiple obstacles. Agents are assigned start and goal locations and must navigate through a shared roadmap while avoiding collisions and minimizing path cost. Each simulation is run under three communication-limited strategies: ¾-WSNE, Two-Way ¾-WSNE, and ⅔-WSNE. The ¾-WSNE approach, which involves no communication, often results in collisions due to random strategy selection. In contrast, the Two-Way ¾-WSNE allows limited information exchange, significantly reducing collision likelihood and improving path quality. The ⅔-WSNE algorithm, benefiting from slightly more communication, consistently produces paths that match or approximate the shortest collision-free solutions, highlighting the effectiveness of approximation equilibria in constrained environments.

We validate our coordination framework through field tests using two Autonomous Surface Vehicles (ASVs) operating in a real-world aquatic environment. Each ASV is assigned start and goal positions on a predefined roadmap and executes paths based on strategies computed using the ¾-WSNE, Two-Way ¾-WSNE, and ⅔-WSNE algorithms. In the ¾-WSNE trials, limited decision-making due to lack of communication often leads to suboptimal or collision-prone paths. The Two-Way ¾-WSNE algorithm enables minimal information sharing, allowing agents to avoid collisions while navigating less efficient but safer routes. The ⅔-WSNE algorithm, which incorporates slightly more communication, produces consistently safe and more balanced paths for both agents. These results demonstrate the real-world viability of our game-theoretic coordination strategies under communication constraints.
